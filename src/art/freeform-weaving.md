Basket weaving is one of the oldest ways of making a curved surface from flat
strips. Traditionally the curvature is forced in by inserting defects, swapping a
hexagon for a pentagon, which makes the shape faceted and abrupt. Here the
curvature is coaxed instead: by giving each ribbon a gentle curve before it is
woven, the surface bends smoothly and continuously, with no defects at all.

What draws us to it is that the form is almost entirely **geometric**. The shape a
weave settles into is set by the shape of its ribbons, not by force or fastening.
Lay out the ribbons correctly and the surface finds its own equilibrium: a dome, a
saddle, a vessel, held together only by the friction of the crossings.

These pieces are woven by hand from laser-cut ribbons, working between the
craft tradition and the computational design that lets us decide, in advance, the
exact curve of every strip.

## Notes

This is the gallery companion to the lab's research on
[woven structures](/projects/woven-structures/), where the same ideas are
developed quantitatively. Here the interest is in the objects themselves: how they
sit, how light passes through the open lattice, and the strange smoothness of a
curved surface made entirely from straight-edged strips.
